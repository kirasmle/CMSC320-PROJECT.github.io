Introduction

The overall objective of this project will be to analyze crypto currency in the stock market from March 2025 to present day. We hope to answer our primary question in order to help people make money!

Cryptocurrency markets have been defined by high volatility, rapid price swings, and inconsistent signals about long term sustainability. Since their inception, cryptocurrency markets have been extremely difficult to predict. For individual investors, this creates a challenging environment: while large gains are possible, so are large and rapid losses, and distinguishing meaningful trends from meaningless market noise is difficult for any human. To investigate whether short-term market data can guide investment decisions, we analyze a cryptocurrency dataset from Kaggles Crypto and Stock Market Data dataset, which provides detailed information on various metrics that we will attempt to use to predict the movement of cryptocurrencies.

The central question motivating our project is: Can we predict whether a cryptocurrency will experience a positive or negative price change in the next 24 hours using recent market indicators?

If such predictions are even moderately reliable, they could help investors decide whether a given cryptocurrency is worth entering, avoiding, or monitoring more closely. Conversely, if the models fail to outperform random guessing, that result would indicate that short-term crypto movements are effectively unpredictable for practical investment purposes, and that investors should not attemt to find trends when they do not exist, and can use other criteria to inform their investing strategies.

This question is important because it tests a common assumption in the crypto community. This assumption is that past momentum, trading volume, or market cap trends can signal short term gains or losses. By cleaning and structuring the dataset, performing exploratory analysis and hypothesis testing, and training machine learning models on recent market features, we aim to evaluate whether these indicators contain actionable information for decision making or amount to little more than just noise. Our analysis ultimately seeks to give readers a clearer understanding of how predictable (or unpredictable) short term crypto performance truly is,, and whether or not data science can help inform our investing choices.

Data Processing


Next, we will need to parse through the data in order to make conversions that will be necesssary to analyze our data. First we need to convert the string timestamp object into the timestamp type. Next we need to convert dollars to float, as some of the data points contain T, B, M, and K indicating the number is represented in the trillions, billions, millions, or thousands respectively. Additionally, we convert percentages to floats by dividing by 100. We apply these changes to our df to create the cleaned_df


















In[]:






def dollars_to_float(series: pd.Series) -> pd.Series:
    s = series.astype(str)
    s = s.mask(s.str.contains('%', na=False))
    s = s.str.replace(r'[\$,]', '', regex=True)
    s = s.str.replace(r'([0-9\.]+)T', lambda m: str(float(m.group(1)) * 1_000_000_000_000), regex=True)
    s = s.str.replace(r'([0-9\.]+)B', lambda m: str(float(m.group(1)) * 1_000_000_000), regex=True)
    s = s.str.replace(r'([0-9\.]+)M', lambda m: str(float(m.group(1)) * 1_000_000), regex=True)
    s = s.str.replace(r'([0-9\.]+)K', lambda m: str(float(m.group(1)) * 1_000), regex=True)
    return s.astype(float)
def percent_to_float(series: pd.Series) -> pd.Series:
    s = series.astype(str)
    s = s.mask(s.str.contains(r'\$', na=False))
    s = s.str.replace('%', '', regex=True)
    s = s.astype(float) / 100
    return s

Primary Analysis






















The raw dataset contains snapshots of many cryptocurrencies at irregular intervals (approximately every 1030 minutes), with fields such as price_usd, chg_24h, and chg_7d. However, these percentage changes tells us information about the past. They describe how much the price has moved in the last 24 hours or 7 days and tell us nothing about the future. In order to create a machine learning algorithm to predict future cryptocurrency prices, we need to introduce future looking data. To do this, we will create a new column in our dataframe which will tell us what the price of the cryuptocurrency ended up being roughly 24 hours after the datum was collected.


Here, we now need to create a df to hold all of our data, with current market indicators as well as what the price ended up being 24 hours later. This will help us create a machine learning model that can use past example of market facts and prices along with their future prices in order to predict future prices with current market indicators. We begin by create a dataframe that contains the price of each row, as close to 24 hours in the future as possible. We will then merge that dataframe with the cleaned_df in order to create a new binary column, with 0s and 1s, to represent whether or not the price went up or down 24 hours later. 0 represents that the price did not go up, while 1 represents that the price did indeed go up.

# sort by coin name then the time
ml_df = ml_df.sort_values(['name', 'timestamp'])

# find the timestamp that would be 24 hours later
ml_df['timestamp_24h'] = ml_df['timestamp'] + pd.Timedelta(hours=24)

# here we are creating a new df that has the information about the data in 24 hours
future_df = ml_df[['name', 'timestamp', 'price_usd']].rename(columns={'timestamp': 'timestamp_future', 'price_usd': 'price_usd_24h'})

# we use merge_asof() in order to align each row with the first timestamp that is 24 hours into the future of the current row's timestamp
df_merged = pd.merge_asof(ml_df.sort_values('timestamp_24h'),future_df.sort_values('timestamp_future'),by='name',left_on='timestamp_24h',right_on='timestamp_future',direction='forward')

# we need to drop any rows that do not have a corresponding row 24 hours into the future
df_merged = df_merged.dropna(subset=['price_usd_24h'])

# we calculate the flag that tells us if the future price went up (1) or did not (0). This is a binary label that will help us trian our machine learning model
df_merged['target_up_24h'] = (df_merged['price_usd_24h'] > df_merged['price_usd']).astype(int)

























Before we move on to the actual creation of the machine learning model, let us take a look at the dsitribution of the class, or flag. What this means is that we are going to look at what percent of the times the future (24 hour in the future price) ends up being a 0 (same or lower) or a 1 (higher price). This will tell us if there is an imbalance in the label. When there are imbalances that are not accounted for, the machine learning model that we create can have a bias towards the more common flag rather than actually using the data that we want it to make the decisions off of. This can lead to issues where test data that does not share the same imbalance as the training data causes the model to perform very poorly.

Machine Learning Model Training


For our question, we have created a binary flag. This flag, either a 0 or 1, tells us whether the price of the cryptocurrency did or did not end up going up 24 hours later.


We decided to use Random Forest as our machine learning model. Random Forest, unlike Logistic Regression, does not use a linear baseline. This allows us to capture non-linear interactions between variables. This can help us combine the features to create accurate predictions. Despite this, we will also create a logistic regression classifier so that we can compare the results of the two models, and then create some graphs to capture the results and understand the models.


We will compare the accuracy of these two machine learning models in order to determine which one is more accurate than the other, and then figure out which one would be better. This will help us understand how the features that we have selected really work together to potentially predict future cryptocurrency prices.

On the test set, both Logistic Regression and Random Forest achieve accuracies around 70%, with Random Forest being slighlty higher. These prices change tremendously from day to day, often for no apparent reason. This can make predictions off of limited market indicators and without social context very difficult.
